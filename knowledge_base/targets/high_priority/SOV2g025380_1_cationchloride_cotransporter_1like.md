### 1. FUNCTION: Cation-chloride cotransporter 1-like

**KNOWN:**
Cation-chloride cotransporters (CCCs) are a family of integral membrane proteins found across eukaryotes. They mediate the electroneutral co-transport of chloride (Cl-) with cations, typically potassium (K+) and/or sodium (Na+), across biological membranes. In animal systems, CCCs (e.g., NKCC1, KCCs) are well-characterized for their roles in cell volume regulation, transepithelial ion transport, and neuronal excitability.

In plants, CCCs are less extensively studied compared to animals, but homologs exist. *Arabidopsis thaliana* has several genes annotated as CCCs (e.g., AtCCC, AtKCC1). These plant CCCs are thought to be involved in:
*   **Ion homeostasis**: Maintaining appropriate intracellular concentrations of K+, Na+, and Cl-.
*   **Osmoregulation and turgor maintenance**: By regulating cellular ion content, CCCs influence water potential and turgor, which is crucial for cell expansion and movement (e.g., stomatal regulation).
*   **Long-distance transport**: Potentially involved in loading/unloading ions into/from xylem and phloem.
*   **Stress responses**: Participation in salt stress tolerance by regulating Na+ and Cl- accumulation or exclusion.

**INFERRED/UNCERTAIN:**
The "1-like" in the annotation suggests homology to a specific CCC subtype, possibly NKCC1 (Na-K-2Cl cotransporter 1) or KCC1 (K-Cl cotransporter 1) from animal systems, or a plant-specific CCC1. Without more detailed phylogenetic analysis or experimental characterization of SOV2g025380.1, its precise substrate specificity (e.g., Na+/K+/Cl- vs. K+/Cl-), transport directionality (influx vs. efflux), and subcellular localization (e.g., plasma membrane, tonoplast) in spinach remain inferred from general CCC knowledge.
